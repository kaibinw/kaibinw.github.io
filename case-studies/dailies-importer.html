The studio problem

Ask any VFX editor about their mornings. Overnight, artists publish renders into ShotGrid across hundreds of shots — animation blocking, lighting takes, comp versions, matte-painting passes — and before the day's first review, editorial has to find what's new, pull it in, and rebuild dailies timelines the supervisor can actually scrub. On a busy show that's hours of setup, every single day, multiplied by however many departments are reviewing. It's the most repetitive job in editorial and the most punishing to get wrong: a missed render is a shot that doesn't get reviewed, and everyone in the room notices.

The fix isn't importing faster. It's never rebuilding: conform the cut once, then only ever append what changed.

What it does

Takes an EDL onceThe cut comes in as an EDL a single time and becomes the master review timeline. That's the whole setup cost. From then on the tool maintains the timelines; nobody rebuilds them by hand again.
Scan by departmentWalks the show's render folders and classifies new media by which department published it: animation, lighting, compositing, matte painting, editorial temps. Frame sequences and movie files both.
Config as memoryEach department lives in a JSON config with an on/off toggle. Newly discovered departments are added disabled: the tool never imports something it hasn't been told to want.
Append, don't rebuildA timeline registry remembers which review timeline each department feeds. Every run appends only what's new — the hours of daily setup collapse into the seconds the import takes.
Colour as languageEvery imported clip gets a Resolve colour tag by department, so the timeline reads at a glance in review: who published it, and how far along it is.

Under the hood it's three panels over one shared config and one EDL registry: a Scan & Import for the overnight batch, a Quick Import for the single render an artist just published, and a Department Import that governs what any scan is allowed to touch. An EDL-aware render panel is the next one being built.

The morning batch: scan and append

Quick import: one render, placed on the right track

Most of the day isn't a full scan. An artist pings that one shot is re-rendered and it needs to be in the review timeline now. Quick Import takes exactly one thing: the path. Paste a frame path or a version folder and it parses the sequence, shot, department, and version, sniffs EXR sequence versus movie file, and shows precisely what it's about to bring in before you commit to anything.

The part I lean on every day is what happens next. The tool looks the shot up in the EDL registry, reads its record-in, drops the clip onto the first clear video track at that exact timeline position, and stacks the new version as a layer above the old one. No scrubbing to find the shot, no manual trim: the EDL already told it where the shot lives.

Department import: the whitelist, by department

"What counts as a render worth importing" is different on every show, so nothing is hardcoded. The tool sorts every render it has ever seen into departments — Animation, Lighting, Compositing, Editorial — so you whitelist at the level you actually think in: turn Compositing on for EXR, leave Lighting movie files off, per format. Anything new it discovers arrives as a department switched off, logged, and waiting for a human yes.

On deck: an EDL-aware render tab

The same registry that places clips knows every shot's in and out to the frame, which makes the obvious next panel almost free. Pick an export preset and the render tab dials each shot's range straight from the EDL — no trimming, no guessed handles — then queues them all at once and hands the batch to Resolve's own render engine. Multiple shots, multiple presets, one click. Designed and being built; the EDL parsing and placement it stands on already ship.

What it demonstrates

  • Editorial knowledge is the spec. The department breakdown, the timeline structure, and the colour language encode how reviews actually run. A pipeline TD could write this script; only an editor would know what it should do.
  • Config-driven means show-portable. Point it at a different show root, hand it that show's EDL, let it discover the render types, toggle on what matters. Nothing is hardcoded to one production.
  • Trust through restraint. Unknown media is surfaced but never auto-imported, and reruns are idempotent. The tool earns its place in a daily-deadline workflow by being boring and predictable.
  • One good data model pays off more than once. The EDL registry was built to place clips on the right track. That same record in/out is what makes instant version-layering work and what makes a frame-accurate render tab nearly free. Get the spine right and the features get cheap.

Back to the studio problem

  • The morning scramble is already solved — this one is studio work. The hours-per-day setup cost in the opening isn't hypothetical; this tool removed it from a working editorial department, and a missed render now surfaces as a flagged unknown instead of an empty slot in review.
  • Portability is the proof. Because the show root, the EDL, and the render types are all config, the same core answers the next show's morning too; the setup cost of the whole system is one EDL.
